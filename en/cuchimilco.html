Cuchimilco

Description

The cuchimilcos are solid clay statuettes, decorated with pigments, representing human figures with their arms short and extended as if ready to fly or inviting an embrace. They can be naked, half-naked or dressed, but in any case, they show the genitals, distinguishing their masculine or feminine condition. It belongs to the Chancay culture, which developed between 1200 and 1470 in the central coast of Peru, and had its center in the current province of Huaral, in the region of Lima.

A popular version affirms that Cuchimilco derives from cuchi (name that the natives of Peru gave to the pig brought by the Spaniards), because the archaeological sites where the statuettes were found were close to pig farms. Another peculiarity is that the features of the face are painted, as well as a headdress or cap on the head, on which there are holes to place feathers.

Relevant Data

  • Most of the cuchimilcos were found in the tombs of the Chancay culture. In the oldest tombs they are usually found in pairs, male and female, but in the later ones only female representations appear.

  • Inspired by the cuchimilcos, designer Andrea Medrano created Milco, a fictitious character that won the contest to choose the mascot for the 2019 Pan American Games, to be held in Lima. What motivated the designer for its creation was the peculiar position of open arms of these statuettes, which she reinterpreted as the hospitality that Peru offers to foreigners, in addition to belonging to a culture of Norte Chico (north of Lima).
